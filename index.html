About this tool

The finding. At frame 212 (t = 42.2 s), Qwen2.5-VL asserts "no visible construction barriers." Alpamayo R1 says "Nudge to the right to pass the construction cone in the lane." The encroachment is real — it's a restaurant parklet — but neither model says so. In a 2.6-second window from 40.2 s to 42.8 s, Alpamayo flags the obstacle in six frames (202, 203, 204, 212, 213, 215), labeling it differently each time (barrier → barricade → sign → cone → cone → line). Qwen says "no construction barriers" or doesn't mention obstruction. Two failure modes from two training paradigms, side by side. Action training reshapes attention. A VLA isn't a VLM with an action head bolted on — it's a model where what counts as salient is defined by what triggers an action. Cones trigger nudges. Cones get attended to.

What it is. Alpamayo R1 (NVIDIA, 10B-param VLA) sampled at 5 Hz on a 44-second San Francisco unprotected-left clip. Each frame produces (1) a terse action-justification from Alpamayo and (2) a 50-word scene narration from Qwen2.5-VL-72B (via Nebius). 220 frames, paired.

Honest caveat. Ego pose was recovered via dense optical flow with a constant-velocity prior — not metric. This evaluates the model's per-frame reasoning behavior on a YouTube clip; it's not a closed-loop driving demo. See model card for what Alpamayo is and isn't.

About the trajectory. The green line is what Alpamayo's planner outputs given the ego-pose history we feed it. Here, that pose comes from optical flow on a YouTube dashcam — wobbly and unit-arbitrary (no GPS, no IMU). Same model on a clip with real ego pose produces smooth, monotonic trajectories. The wobble you see is the input's fault, not the model's — action quality in a VLA is bounded by proprioception quality. Toggle off by default for clarity.
